# Robonomics — October 15, 2024

Investor interest in robotics is skyrocketing—and it’s easy to see why. But there are still many misconceptions, and few people realize just how early we are in this journey.

While we all hope for a “ChatGPT moment” in robotics, I’m afraid we’re not there yet.

🤖 Hardware: Where Things Actually Stand

$TSLA’s Optimus stirred plenty of debate after Robotaxi Day. But here’s the thing:

Showcasing 20–30 biped robots is a big deal.

It signals that Tesla can now produce a few robots per week, with consistent quality.

Their hardware production looks polished—and they've likely mastered the supply chain fundamentals.

What impressed me:

The robots' walking and balancing were smooth—none fell

They maintained stability while moving arms and under varied temperatures

Tesla had the confidence to let them walk around a live crowd of 3,000 people

That alone should make history.

And the tele-op complaints?

Yes, some critics focused on the teleoperated hands and speech.

But remote operation at that level of dexterity and low latency, across that distance, is already impressive.

With a live audience of thousands, Tesla likely chose the safest backup option available.

💰 What About Cost?

Looking ahead, I believe humanoid robots could eventually cost $20–30k.

Why?

A humanoid robot: ~1,000 components, ~150 lbs

An EV: ~10,000 components, ~4,000–5,000 lbs

A well-designed humanoid robot should be cheaper to build than a car. That’s the direction we’re heading.

🧠 AI & Data: Still Early, Still Starving

There’s a lot of talk about AI breakthroughs in robotics—but we’re still very early.

Robot models are heavily data-starved, and we haven’t yet seen true world models or generalization emerge.

Great thread from @BradPorter_

link

He compares robotics today to self-driving 20 years ago.

In the beginning, gains were fast. But after $160B invested over two decades, only one company offers real self-driving rides—in just two cities.

Early training brings fast progress. Real-world performance? Much harder.

Even small improvements in real-world data can yield big jumps—but it’s a slow grind, not a leap.

📊 Why Data Quality Still Rules

Just like in self-driving, people try to hack around the data bottleneck.

But the truth is clear:

You need massive, high-quality, real-world data.

Robotics will face the same challenge.

▶️ Final Thoughts

I know this might sound basic—or even a bit discouraging.

Celebrating robots that walk, balance, and move smoothly?

Being excited about producing a few with consistent quality?

Yes.

Because this is where we are today.

The same goes for robotics AI models—we’re still laying the groundwork for what’s possible.

And yes:

Every step forward is worth celebrating.
