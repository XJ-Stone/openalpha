# Robonomics — October 23, 2024

The rate of improvement in $TSLA’s FSD disengagement ratio is stunning—

It’s direct evidence that scaling laws apply not just to language models but also to real-world autonomy.

[IMAGE: Image]

Let’s break it down:

v13: 5–6x improvement in miles per critical intervention (mpci) (Tesla 3Q Earnings Call)

v12.5: 3x improvement (Source: Tesla AI @Tesla_AI)

v12.4: 5–10x better than v12.3 (Source: @elonmusk)

vs. Jan 2024 (v12.1): 100x improvement in v12.5; 1,000x in v13 (Tesla 3Q Call)

By 2Q25: Tesla expects FSD to be safer than a human driver

▶️ Human vs. AI Driving

Human driver: ~550k miles per reported accident

(3.3 trillion miles / 6 million accidents annually in the U.S.)

This comes out to ~60k miles per minor incident, or an accident every ~3 years at 13.5k miles/year.

Waymo: ~17k miles per disengagement (California DMV 2023)

(The true number may be higher today, but it’s not publicly disclosed.)

Tesla FSD (my estimate):

v12.1 (Jan 2024): ~50 mpci

v12.5: ~5k mpci

v13: 10–20k+ mpci

By 2Q25: ~100k mpci

This scaling is unprecedented—powered by more data, better hardware (HW5/AI5), and more training capacity.

⏳ A Few Thoughts + Open Questions

1/ Why does $TSLA need a Cybercab if Model 3/Y can drive autonomously?

Cost & adoption.

Autonomy still feels futuristic to many. A steering-wheel-free vehicle that looks like the future is the best advertisement. Plus, post-10/10, Tesla saw a meaningful jump in FSD attach rate.

Cybercab will likely cost ~$25k, with 5x faster assembly cycles and fewer parts. It’s the right vehicle to scale robotaxi economics.

2/ Why do public trackers report only ~200 mpci?

Because humans over-intervene.

Most tracker data reflects user-reported disengagements. When you’re in the driver’s seat, your threshold for taking over is low—you feel responsible.

Compare that to riding in a $Waymo as a passenger: you don’t see the wheel, you’re not ready to brake, and you mentally disengage. “Out of sight, out of mind.”

3/ Why start the robotaxi fleet with Model 3/Y?

Speed and regulation.

These vehicles are already crash-tested, produced at scale, and approved in most regions. With autonomy laws varying state by state, using known vehicles gives $TSLA more flexibility to adapt and scale.

⏳ Other Thoughts

🚗 Will robotaxis kill car ownership?

Possibly both:

Pro-ownership: Lower cost of ownership + cars generating income

Anti-ownership: If autonomous fleets are everywhere, why own a car?

The balance will depend on urban density, local regulation, and convenience.

🚛 Autonomous trucking = major disruption

Elon hinted that Tesla Semi will also go autonomous.

The implications for logistics, freight, and labor are massive—lower costs, faster delivery, and big shifts in employment across transportation sectors.

⚖️ Regulation is still a mess

Why are we still doing state-by-state approvals for something so transformative?

We need federal-level frameworks for autonomy, just like we did for seatbelts or crash testing. It’s too important to leave fragmented.

The Bottom Line

$TSLA has taken heat for years over FSD delays. But we may now be at a tipping point. The scaling math is starting to look exponential—and if Tesla pulls this off, it’s not just a car company.

When 2M–4M Cybercabs hit the roads, it’ll feel like sci-fi made real—armies of robots reshaping mobility, labor, and infrastructure.

It’s not just autonomy—it’s real-world AI at scale. And the future’s arriving fast.

This was one of the best earnings calls from $TSLA in recent memory. The clarity of thought—deeply rooted in first-principles thinking—was exceptional. Two moments stood out:

1/ Lower Cost = Higher Adoption

It sounds simple, but this idea is the foundation of nearly everything Tesla and Elon pursue.

On the call, Tesla laid out the roadmap—the marriage of EV and AI strategies—and it all funnels down to a single principle:

Drive down the cost of vehicles (whether owned or autonomous) to drive up adoption of sustainable transportation.

In the world of autonomy, it’s all about cost per mile—not performance, not luxury. The cheapest vehicle to operate wins.

[IMAGE: Image]

2/ Devil’s in the Details: Engineering is Relentless

Elon explained why cutting 20% off a car’s cost is far harder than it sounds. It’s not about tweaking a few components—it’s a full redesign of the vehicle and factory as a system.

Every detail counts:

Shaving pennies off each of the 10,000+ parts

Reducing part count wherever possible

Rethinking production from the ground up

“If any part is done in a silo, it becomes a bottleneck for cost, time, or efficiency.”

This kind of systems-level cost engineering is what sets Tesla apart. It’s not just about building cars—it’s about redesigning how we build them.
