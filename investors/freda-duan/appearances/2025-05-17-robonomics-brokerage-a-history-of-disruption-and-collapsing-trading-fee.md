# Robonomics — May 17, 2025

Love this (O3-generated) chart—wild to see how dramatically trading fees have fallen:

–85% in 8 years ($14 per trade → $2)

–95% since early 2000s ($30–40 per trade → ~$2 or free)

[IMAGE: chart/figure]

💥 What Drove the Collapse?

Each drop wasn’t random. Two forces reshaped the market:

Technology shifts – from computerized routing → internet → mobile

Bold disruptors – $SCHW in 1975, $HOOD in 2015

📈 Equity Ownership Rose With Every Wave

1980s: <20% of U.S. households owned equities

Early 2000s: ~40%

2015: ~52%

Today: ~62%

🕰 Key Moments in History

🔹 1975 – “May Day” Deregulation & the Birth of Discount Brokers

Tech shift: Computerized order routing + 800-number access

Regulatory unlock: May Day ended fixed commissions

Disruptor: $SCHW (launched 1975)

$SCHW slashed full-service fees 70% by cutting out research, reports, and in-branch advisors.

Execution-only trading ($30–$40 flat) undercut the 2% commission model.

It scaled nationally via tech while legacy firms stayed branch-based.

Merrill reacted by launching Merrill Lynch Direct and pivoting to wrap accounts (AUM-based fees).

🔹 Mid-1990s–2010s – 401(k)s, Web 1.0 Trading, Price War 1.0

Tech shift: Internet adoption

Demand driver: 401(k) boom brought millions into markets

Disruptor: E*TRADE (launched 1992)

By the early 2000s:

Online trades: ~$29.95

Phone trades: ~$65

Self-directed trading exploded.

Investors abandoned broker-assisted trades for web-based platforms.

$SCHW kicked off Price War 1.0 (2004–05), slashing fees to stay ahead of E*TRADE, Ameritrade, Scottrade.

🔹 2017–2019 – Mobile Apps, $0 Commission, Price War 2.0

Tech shift: Mobile-first UX

Disruptor: $HOOD (launched 2013, mobile-only, zero commission)

$HOOD made trading feel “free” (thanks to PFOF).

It pressured incumbents into their final pricing collapse:

Feb 28, 2017: Fidelity cut commissions to $4.95 (–38%)

$SCHW matched within hours

E*TRADE, TD Ameritrade followed within days

By 2019: $0 commissions were standard

In just 30 years, trading transformed from a gated, high-cost service into a zero-friction, mass-market utility.

📌 Final Thought

Every wave of disruption made trading faster, cheaper, and more accessible. But each wave also shifted who holds the power—first from brokers to platforms, and now potentially to algorithms and AI agents.

Fee compression isn’t over. The next battle will be about who owns the user, who owns the data, and who automates the advice.

We’ve collapsed commissions. The next frontier?
