# Robonomics — September 05, 2025

Partnerships: What Makes more Sense, Avis type or Uber/Lyft type

The Avis deal isn’t a surprise (Waymo first partnered with Avis in 2017). The model is clear:

Waymo runs service, routing, and dispatch.

Avis runs depots and readiness (cleaning, charging, parking, maintenance). Makes a lot sense given Avis already has the local teams/ parking lots in major cities.

Avis has signaled it’s willing to “put our balance sheet to work” for future vehicle purchases—hinting at an asset-heavy revenue stream (depreciation spread, financing fees).

Strategic logic:

Uber partnership → faster ramp in new cities.

Avis partnership → long-term margin capture + brand control.

Possible playbook: seed demand with Uber; then transition to Waymo-app + Avis ops as awareness builds. Maybe even some form of rev share with the operating partner/ fleet owners

Dallas (2025–26): Multi-year deal, Avis handles fleet ops + depots; Waymo runs app and self-driving stack. Public launch in 2026.

Phoenix (2017): Avis supported early rider program with cleaning, charging, and storage.

World Model Prediction 

I’m increasingly convinced that robotics and self-driving will ultimately require a world model—one capable of understanding the full spectrum of edge cases, from a burst water pipe to a sudden mountain fire. Today’s self-driving systems, whether Waymo or Tesla (whose approaches are gradually converging), continue to “scale up” and patch code to handle edge cases. But edge cases are infinite by nature. The only real solution is intelligence that understands why things work, not just how drivers usually react. It’s the difference between mimicking a stop sign response and grasping what a stop sign means and why vehicles stop for it.

We’ve seen this dynamic in other domains. Image models, video models, coding agents—over time, the largest general models (LLMs) outperformed specialized vertical ones. I expect the same trajectory here. Latency and edge-compute limits are real questions, but solvable in the long run.

This doesn’t make current approaches irrelevant. Waymo already runs over 250,000 paid rides per week (~13M annually). The cars aren’t flawless—no self-driving system is—but they’re usable at scale. Progress will keep compounding until, eventually, we reach the breakthrough of a true world model.

TAM

I’ve asked myself ten thousand times whether it’s even worth tracking self-driving and robotics. TSLA is not an easy stock, and the whole industry still feels like a “hit or miss,” weighed down by real technical risks. But when you zoom out, the prize is too big to ignore: 3 trillion miles driven every year in the US × $0.60/mile = a $1.8T market. The logic is sound—having a robot drive the car clearly boosts productivity—and we already see Waymo and others proving that it works. Yes, there are still edge cases, and driving is unforgiving (it has to be near 0% risk). It probably makes sense that at some point there will be a world model for driving and robotics.
