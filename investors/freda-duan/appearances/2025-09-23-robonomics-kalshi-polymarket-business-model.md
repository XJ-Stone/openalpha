# Robonomics — September 23, 2025

Investing in Disruptive Tech

Tech investing is about durable growth; the steepest curves come from disruptors. Every few years a “new species” shows up — — first mocked, then massive, and eventually pushing incumbents off the board.

Early on, these looked “wrong”: killed commissions; leaned into crypto when it looked fringe; $NFLX spent through skepticism; ran ultra-subsidized low prices; bet the house on GPUs before AI demand was obvious. Violating consensus created non-linear upside. Once user flywheels synced with capital, they compounded to $100B-scale and rewrote the rules.

What pattern to spot (three tells):

Self-propelled growth: minimal education, viral adoption (trading-as-an-app; short-form creation/consumption).

Extensible TAM: not trapped in a narrow vertical.

Inevitability in hindsight: smartphones → mobile-first distribution; GPUs → AI; new demand in under-served segments → commerce explosions.

By this scorecard, prediction markets stand out: feral user growth, an almost unbounded “everything-is-tradable” TAM, and strong cultural/regulatory tailwinds. More than a line of business, they can become the next knowledge platform—how we surface truth and price the future. This may be the next generational wave taking shape in real time.

What is Prediction Market

Prediction markets aren’t new—they’ve existed throughout history. As early as 1503, people were betting on papal elections. By the late 19th and early 20th centuries, U.S. presidential races were actively traded on Wall Street, with odds printed on newspaper front pages. In other words, the human urge to “predict the future and bet on it” is a centuries-old cultural and psychological need. Phrases like “what are the odds” or “I bet” show how deeply this impulse is built into language and culture.

Mechanically, a prediction market is a “future-facing exchange.” Contracts are tied to event outcomes, priced between $0 and $1, and reflect the real-time, market-implied probability of those events. Because participants put real money at stake, the market turns abstract expectations into a continuously updating signal. Versus traditional polling, the incentives are more aligned and the feedback loop is faster.

The turning point came in the 2024 U.S. election cycle. In October, mainstream polls still called the race a toss-up, while Polymarket had already priced Trump’s win probability above 60%. That gap pushed prediction markets into the global mainstream—not as a fringe novelty, but as a tool that can “surface the truth.”

Today, prediction markets reach far beyond politics: traders can bet on whether TSLA beats earnings, whether the Fed cuts by 25 or 50 bps, who the next U.S. president will be, even whether Taylor Swift will be pregnant in 2025. These seemingly disparate questions trade on the same platform with real liquidity. That makes prediction markets look less like mere gambling venues and more like the next-generation information platform.

The Scale Is Getting Real

Before 2024, Kalshi and Polymarket had near-zero volume; now they’re running close to a $40B annualized pace—about half of HOOD’s crypto trading volume. Kalshi grew over 80% QoQ in a single quarter, matching the “wild growth” profile of a new species.

HOOD’s distribution role is pivotal: prediction-market volume topped $1B in Q2; at $0.01 per contract, HOOD is on a $40M annualized fee run rate. On NCAA football’s opening weekend alone, HOOD cleared 100M football contracts. More importantly, Kalshi’s 3Q25 volume is 2× the 2024 election quarter, signaling the surge isn’t a one-off election trade but a genuine move into the mainstream.

Culturally, prediction markets still carry a rebellious streak: Polymarket was fined by the CFTC and founder Shayne Coplan faced investigation; Kalshi’s founders are classic MIT nerds. Yet mainstream finance and tech are converging fast:

HOOD and COIN are leaning into prediction markets; COIN even pitched an “Everything Exchange” spanning crypto, tokenized equities, stablecoins, and prediction markets. IBKR launched ForecastEx;

X named Polymarket its official prediction partner; xAI tied Grok’s AI analysis to Kalshi odds;

Bloomberg pipes Polymarket election odds into the terminal;

Sports-betting giant FLUT (FanDuel) teamed with CME to launch regulated event contracts in the U.S.; DKNG is exploring moves too.

In short, prediction markets mix “outsider rebellion” with “insider embrace”—an edge innovation now supercharged by incumbents.

Polymarket vs. Kalshi

Polymarket: decentralized, on Polygon/Ethereum; smart-contract + stablecoin settlement; user base largely ex-U.S. Recently bought licensed derivatives venue QCEX for a U.S. push.

Kalshi: centralized and fully compliant with a CFTC DCM license; strict KYC/AML; U.S.-centric users. A key edge is distribution via HOOD. September’s spike owed much to HOOD listing NFL/NCAA contracts in Aug–Sep and adding sportsbook-like point spreads and totals.

Primary-market valuations show the split: Polymarket ~$9B, Kalshi ~$5B. Investors tend to award Polymarket a premium for global narrative and buzz. If HOOD vs. COIN is your mental model: Polymarket resembles the “rule-bending crowd-pleaser,” while Kalshi mirrors the “by-the-book” route. Both are converging: Polymarket is entering the U.S.; Kalshi is pushing crypto/chain integrations (e.g., Solana/Base) to expand beyond the U.S. and plug into on-chain ecosystems.

Why This is Exciting - Knowledge Market of the World

Looking back at tech history, each rise of a “new species” always feels abrupt in the moment, yet post-hoc reviews feel almost fated. HOOD didn’t appear out of thin air; it perfectly captured the mobile-internet generation’s “won’t pay, prefer free” consumer culture. UBER wasn’t an accident; GPS plus smartphones happened to satisfy the most basic human need—certainty from point A to point B. PDD’s explosion is a classic “structural gap,” quickly taking root amid a traffic shortage for long-tail merchants and a supply shortage for lower-tier users.

The post-2024-election rise of prediction markets is similarly inevitable. Multiple forces are at work:

Culture & trust crisis: Mainstream media credibility has sunk; “fake news” is routine. People want tools closer to truth than polls and headlines. Polymarket’s higher-than-poll Trump pricing during the election offered evidence. Prediction markets are no longer just betting—they’re social tools against uncertainty and false narratives.

Information asymmetry & market mapping: The world is getting more “leaky.” From tariffs to company financials, someone always seems to know early, and human nature turns that into trades. Prediction markets are the most direct stage for this “early-info” behavior. Real money at stake makes them closer to underlying facts than polls.

Retail revolution & distribution base: In the U.S., 62% of stock market participants are retail. They’re used to expressing views in markets and spreading sentiment on social media. Prediction market charts look a lot like stock charts, making them a natural extension for HOOD’s user base. It’s not just trading—it’s the democratization of “participating in the future.”

Regulatory arbitrage & institutional gap: Sports betting is regulated at the state level, with 12 major states (e.g., California, Texas) banning it outright. Prediction markets, however, fall under federal CFTC oversight, opening the national market at once. This institutional gap lets prediction markets enter previously off-limits territory “legally.”

Policy climate & timing window: “This is an administration for you to play offense, not defense.” This administration has green-lit fast lanes for assets long in the gray zone—especially important in the U.S. What if policy tightens in four years? Many disruptive innovations rely on such windows of “human factors.” If prediction markets complete user education and build scale/liquidity within this window, they can grow wildly before rules fully settle.

Once prediction markets reach scale, the classic network effect kicks in: more users → deeper liquidity → stronger price discovery → attracts more users, forming a moat.

How They Differ From Sportsbooks

Offerings

Prediction markets: Politics, Sports, Crypto, Earnings, Geopolitics, Tech, Culture, Macro themes, rotating groups like “Trump” or “Elections.”

Sportsbooks: Sports.

Regulation & Reach

Sportsbooks: State-by-state. No sportsbooks in CA, TX, GA, etc..

Prediction markets: CFTC-regulated, nationwide availability.

Liquidity

Prediction markets rely on market makers and LPs to provide liquidity. Sportsbooks, by contrast, are the liquidity—they take the other side of your bet and manage risk themselves.

Because each prediction market needs a broader network of participants to function, they build a deeper moat through network effects.

Product Experience

Polymarket / Kalshi: Prices move continuously as traders post or cancel orders. You can enter or exit anytime before resolution, with your P&L floating as the market shifts. Some say it’s overly simplified, but I like how prediction markets strip complex scenarios down into clean, single-factor bets.

DraftKings / FanDuel / Flutter: Odds are fixed the moment you place a bet. Even if the market moves, your ticket doesn’t reprice.

While I don’t have exact data, I’d bet engagement is higher on prediction markets. Prices move constantly, so participants are incentivized to check in all the time—not just when the game ends.

Crypto Rails vs Traditional?

For U.S. consumer scale, big brands, and policy durability: Go traditional/regulated (DCM)—that’s Kalshi today, and Polymarket US (via QCX) soon. More overhead, but legitimacy + distribution.

For global liquidity, fast iteration, and low friction: Crypto remains the fastest path (Polymarket global). Especially outside the U.S. or among crypto-native users. QCX will narrow the U.S. gap.

In just a year, prediction markets have moved from the fringe to the mainstream: election coverage cites Polymarket, X is filled with Kalshi/Polymarket charts, and Bloomberg has integrated the odds. They’re evolving from “retail entertainment” into “information infrastructure.”

Potential forms:

A price layer for facts: Turn dispersed judgments into tradable, subscribable probability curves—the default reference frame for media, research, and institutional decisions (like quotes for stocks).

A knowledge platform: Politics, macro, sports, culture—“everything is tradable”; users don’t just consume information, they stake capital to express views.

API-ified infrastructure: Price feeds, contract creation, settlement, and an “event index” embed via APIs into media, brokers, search, and social—becoming the internet’s probability protocol layer.

Business model: trading fees + data subscriptions + ad funnels + interest income + enterprise/institutional apps. Compared with sports betting’s single 9% take-rate model, prediction markets have a broader monetization path.

Moat: dual network effects (users ↔ liquidity ↔ accuracy), data accumulation, distribution tie-ins, and federal regulatory barriers.

Risks & window: regulation may swing back, but today’s “green-light fast lane” is enough for wild growth. Once past critical mass, network effects can make it as hard to reverse as HOOD/NFLX.

Underlying trend: as RWA blurs fiat/non-fiat and physical/digital, prediction markets are blurring information/asset: information isn’t just “read”—it’s priced, traded, and settled. If successful, prediction markets won’t just be a market but a global “tradable truth layer / Knowledge Market of the World.”
