# Robonomics — October 27, 2025

There’s a growing debate around whether the projected $200B in OpenAI revenue (per The Information) represents new spending or just a reshuffling of existing digital dollars.

[IMAGE: chart/figure]

To think about it clearly, it helps to zoom out:

The past 25 years of the internet have mostly been about mix shift, not market expansion. Total ad spend grew roughly with GDP, but online’s share went from 0% → 70%.

e.g. Today, U.S. ad spend totals about $380B/year, ~70% of which (~$260B) is online.

Within that, Google’s U.S. ad rev is roughly $140B; Meta’s about $70B.

Ads = ~3% of adjusted PCE, meaning that on average, U.S. businesses spend about 3% of total consumption on advertising. (Of course, DTC brands might spend 20%, while Target spends 1%.)

Total “online rev” across all major categories:

Ads: $260B = 3% of adj. PCE

Payments: $180B = 1.5% of card volume

Software (SaaS + Cloud): $850B = 4% of PCE

E-commerce: GMV$1.2T; ~$100B 3P take-rate revenue

Online subscriptions ( , Prime, etc.): $50B

^That’s the digital economy we know— , , , , .

Now layer in AI.

If OpenAI really does $200B in rev—let’s say $100B from the U.S.—where does that money come from?

Few options:

Cannibalize existing “digital spend” (ads $180B + ecomm take-rates $100B). Winner vs. Losers?

Create new markets—basically a new “digital tax” on the $20T U.S. PCE base.

Share-of-savings (from wages): Start with your $15T U.S. labor spend. 

AI automates a slice ⇒ firms save $$ on payroll. Vendors price as “% of savings” → that % becomes AI revenue. Share-of-savings: $15T × 10% automatable × 40% adopted × 20% vendor-take ≈ $120B/yr.

Seats & APIs (software opex): Comes out of companies’ software budgets (SG&A/R&D), not wages per se.

The bull case: AI expands the total pie. Historically, major productivity waves—like IT in the 1990s—drove real PCE growth: goods got cheaper, wages rose, and spending increased.

US Media & Entertainment Industry

“Hollywood + TV + streaming” in the US → ~$150B per year (rough range, depends on definition and whether you count ad revenue separately).

Full US media & entertainment stack (content + ads around it across all formats) → ~$650B per year, heading toward ~$800B.

This includes TV, streaming, film, music, gaming, publishing, plus a lot of advertising around that content.

60% of US “Hollywood/TV/video” money is now online/streaming.

Using the numbers we just anchored on:

Parks Associates pegs the US video services market at about $147B/year (pay TV + streaming subs, US only).

PwC says US OTT/streaming video (SVOD + TVOD etc, not counting all legacy linear TV) was about $84.7B in 2024, projected to hit $112.7B by 2029. TheDesk.net

If you divide:

$84.7B / $147B ≈ 58%

So a good working mental model:

Total US “video services” (TV + streaming) - ~$150B

Of that, online/streaming - ~$80-90B today, so ≈55-60% of the pie, and growing a few points of share every year.

On the usage side, Nielsen has streaming at ~45% of total TV viewing time and still climbing.

Customer Services

A reasonable order-of-magnitude estimate for total US customer service spend is: ≈ $250-350 billion per year

The Eco-system / GDP Calculations:

Setup

Before AI: 100 units of output, 100 workers.

After AI: productivity +10%, firms invest enough in AI gear to actually produce 110 units even with 20% fewer workers (80).

What happens

Real GDP (physical output): +10% → from 100 to 110.

Jobs: −20% (100 → 80). Big displacement at first.

Total wages (economy-wide): up with output → +10% (from 70 to 77 if you assume a 70% labor share).

Pay per worker (for those still employed): +37.5% (because 77 wages / 80 workers vs 70 / 100 before).

Capital income (profits/interest/rents): +10% (from 30 to 33 if capital share is 30%).

Prices / affordability: Productivity makes stuff cheaper (or price growth slower).

If prices barely change, nominal GDP ≈ +10%.

If prices fall ~5% as costs drop, nominal GDP ≈ +4½% (110 units × 0.95 price).

Either way, affordability improves for those with income.

Who buys the extra “10 units”?

Companies do, via the AI capex itself (servers, power, software)—that’s Investment (I).

Households buy more because goods/services are cheaper and remaining workers earn more.

Exports/Govt can absorb some too (NX/G).

Impact

The country makes more stuff, likely at slightly lower prices.

Employed workers and capital owners see clear gains; the aggregate wage pool still rises even with fewer workers.

The pain is distributional: the laid-off 20% need time to retrain and be re-hired into new AI-complement roles.

As re-employment happens, the affordability gains become broad-based.

Scenario 1

Real GDP ≈ 101 (+0.6%).

Wage bill ≈ 70.4 (+0.6%).

Profits ≈ 30.2 (+0.6%).

Wage per worker ≈ +25.8% (0.70 → 0.88).

Interpretation: economy roughly maintains output. Distribution shifts: fewer workers but much higher pay per worker; capital income up. Short-run demand still softer than pre-shock because income moves from high-MPC workers to lower-MPC capital owners and a chunk of workers lose jobs → prices drift down absent policy support.

Scenario 2

Real GDP = 110 (+10%).

Wage bill = 77 (+10%).

Profits = 33 (+10%).

Wage per worker ≈ +37.5% (0.70 → 0.96).

Interpretation: the economy fully realizes the productivity gain despite layoffs. Prices likely fall modestly (or stay stable if the central bank keeps nominal GDP growing). Affordability rises broadly (more goods, cheaper), but you still have a distribution/transition problem: 20% are jobless unless they’re re-employed in new tasks/sectors.
