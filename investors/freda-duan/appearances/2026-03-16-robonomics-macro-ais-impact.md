# Robonomics — March 16, 2026

1/ A Brief History of Mass Layoff Panics

Throughout modern history, societies have repeatedly feared that new technologies or structural shifts would trigger permanent mass unemployment. A few examples:

The Luddites (1811-1850s)

Power looms crushed England’s hand-loom weavers. Wages fell from roughly 40 shillings a week to near zero, and the trade was largely dead by the 1840s. But mechanization also dramatically lowered textile costs and expanded demand, ultimately creating far more total employment across the industry.

American farmers (1800s-1970s)

Farm labor fell from 41% of the U.S. workforce to under 2% - one of the largest occupational shifts in American history. Yet agricultural output more than doubled, and displaced workers were absorbed into manufacturing and, later, services.

Telephone operators (1920s-1960s)

AT&T automated more than 300 cities by 1940. Operator headcount eventually fell from 342,000 at its peak to just 1,460 today. Yet overall female employment did not collapse. Women moved into clerical, administrative, and service roles instead.

ATMs and bank tellers (1970s-2010s)

ATMs reduced the number of tellers needed per branch, from 21 to 13. But because branches became cheaper to operate, banks opened 43% more of them.

The Rust Belt (1980s-present)

This is the darker case. U.S. manufacturing lost 5-6 million jobs in the 2000s alone, devastating places like Flint and Monessen. Only 17% of former manufacturing hubs fully recovered employment - one of the worst records in the developed world.

Radiologists and AI (2016-present)

In 2016, Geoffrey Hinton famously said people should stop training radiologists. Instead, radiology residency positions reached a record high in 2025, and compensation rose 48% to roughly $520K. 

Patterns that keep showing up

1. Specific jobs really do disappear.

2. Aggregate employment usually adjusts - but unevenly.

3. Incumbent workers bear most of the pain.

4. The speed of the transition matters enormously.

Agriculture took a century to unwind. The economy absorbed it. The China shock hit manufacturing regions in less than a decade, and many never recovered. 

5. Education is one of the strongest predictors of resilience.

In nearly every historical episode, more educated workers and cities adapted faster. In the Rust Belt, metro areas with higher college attainment were far more likely to recover.

2/ What Actually Feels Different This Time?

Speed. The agricultural transition from 41% to 2% of the labor force took around a century. Telephone operators took roughly 60 years to fade from relevance. ATMs played out over about 40 years. Even Rust Belt deindustrialization - widely seen as shockingly fast - unfolded over 20-30 years. AI may move much faster.

Generality - maybe? Previous waves mostly automated specific physical tasks within one sector at a time. White-collar work is about 40% of today’s workforce. That is comparable, in scale, to some of the largest historical labor pools that were once seen as vulnerable.

3/ Did Earlier Efficiency Revolutions Cause Deflation or Mass Unemployment?

Sector-specific deflation, yes. But never economy-wide sustained deflation (other than Power Loom which was coupled with post Napoleonic Wars demand collapse).

4/ What Happens if AI Really Does Cause Deflation or Unemployment?

The Fed has four main anti-deflation tools:

Cut interest rates 

QE

Forward guidance

Coordinate with fiscal stimulus - the 2020 playbook

These tools can support aggregate demand. What they cannot do well is determine who benefits.

The most likely macro outcome is something like a "Rust Belt at scale" — nationally, GDP grows, unemployment stays moderate, and no deflation appears in headline numbers. But underneath those aggregates lies tons of inequalities.

Unemployment could rise toward roughly 7-9%

Case 1: The Luddites & the Power Loom (1811–1850s)

English textile workers — skilled hand-loom weavers, stocking-knitters, and croppers — feared that mechanized looms and frames would permanently destroy their livelihoods.

[IMAGE: chart/figure]

Case 2: American Farmers & Mechanization (1800s–1970s)

The fear, recurring across the 19th and 20th centuries, was that tractors, combine harvesters, and other mechanization would leave millions of farm workers destitute. The millions who left farming did not become permanently unemployed — they migrated to cities and entered manufacturing and service jobs. The service sector’s share of employment rose from 31% in 1900 to 78% by 1999.

[IMAGE: chart/figure]

Case 3: Telephone Operators & Automation (1920–1960s)

In the early 1900s, telephone operator was one of the most common jobs for American women — roughly 1 in 13 working women in 1950 was a telephone operator. AT&T’s mechanical switching technology, which allowed users to dial their own calls, threatened to eliminate this entire occupation.

[IMAGE: chart/figure]

Case 4: ATMs & Bank Tellers (1970s–2010s)

When Automated Teller Machines were first introduced in the 1970s, the expectation was obvious: machines that dispense cash and accept deposits will replace the humans who do the same thing. Even President Obama cited ATMs in 2011 as an example of technology displacing labor.

[IMAGE: chart/figure]

Case 5: The Rust Belt & Globalization (1980s–Present)

As NAFTA was signed in 1993 and China joined the WTO in 2001, the fear was that manufacturing jobs would flow out of the American. The national economy did grow — total U.S. employment rose from ~90 million in 1979 to ~159.5 million in 2024 (+80%), driven by service sector expansion. But the gains were not evenly distributed. Cities like Flint (GM employment fell from 80,000 to a fraction), Dayton (lost 15,000 manufacturing jobs), and Monessen (lost two-thirds of its population after steel mills closed) experienced devastating decline.

Case 6: Radiologists

Radiologists were the second-highest-paid medical specialty in 2025, averaging $520,000 — 48% higher than 2015.
