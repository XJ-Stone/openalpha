# Howard Marks

## Background
Howard Marks is the co-founder and co-chairman of Oaktree Capital Management, one of the largest distressed debt and credit investors in the world, managing over $190 billion in assets. He is widely known for his investor memos, which have been published since 1990 and are read by investors globally, including Warren Buffett, who has said "When I see memos from Howard Marks in my mail, they're the first thing I open and read." Marks is the author of "The Most Important Thing" and "Mastering the Market Cycle."

## Investment Style
- Credit and value investor by training, focused on risk management and understanding market cycles
- His core philosophy: "You can't predict the future, but you can prepare for it" through understanding where you are in the cycle
- Emphasizes "second-level thinking" — going beyond the obvious consensus to find where the market is mispricing risk
- Known for his framework of market cycles: oscillation between fear and greed, risk-on and risk-off
- Does not typically pick individual stocks publicly but provides frameworks for evaluating market conditions
- Has increasingly commented on AI and tech valuations, applying his cycle framework to assess whether current enthusiasm is justified

## Current Known Positions (from public statements)
Howard Marks does not publicly discuss specific stock positions in the traditional sense. Oaktree's focus is on credit markets, distressed debt, and alternative investments. However, his commentary on market conditions, risk premiums, and valuation levels is directly relevant to equity investors, particularly regarding tech valuations and AI enthusiasm.

## How to Read Howard Marks
Marks communicates primarily through his Oaktree memos, which are published irregularly (roughly 6-10 per year) and available on Oaktree's website. He also does periodic interviews on Bloomberg, CNBC, and podcast appearances. His memos are essential reading for understanding market cycles, risk, and investor psychology.

When Marks writes about a topic, he brings decades of market experience and a framework anchored in contrarian thinking. He is not an AI investor or a tech specialist, but his commentary on valuation, market cycles, and investor behavior is directly applicable to evaluating whether AI enthusiasm has reached euphoria. His memos titled "Sea Change" (2022) and subsequent updates have been particularly influential in framing the shift to a higher interest rate environment and its implications for asset valuations.
