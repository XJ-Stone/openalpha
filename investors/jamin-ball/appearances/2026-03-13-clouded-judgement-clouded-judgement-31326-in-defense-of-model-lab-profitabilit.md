# Clouded Judgement — March 13, 2026

## Company-Specific Views

### OPENAI (OpenAI) — Bullish, MEDIUM

The author views OpenAI as part of an emerging oligopoly (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google/Gemini) that can achieve classic infra‑style gross margins at scale as serving and architecture efficiencies (prompt caching, auto‑scaling, better utilization) compress inference cost per token. He argues the marginal inference call is "already quite gross margin profitable," and that OpenAI can layer higher‑margin products (applications, ads) atop the model business. Retention and switching costs will rise because enterprises build provider‑specific fine‑tunes, eval suites, prompting patterns, committed spend and agent/tool ecosystems that do not port cleanly. Main risks are failing to stay at the frontier and customers migrating to open‑source stacks for large deployments; catalysts are continued model/platform integration, growing committed spend contracts, and platform features (memory, long‑running workflows) that increase stickiness. Altimeter’s disclosed investment in OpenAI is noted in the publication's disclosure context.

### ANTHROPIC (Anthropic) — Bullish, MEDIUM

The author cites reported margin dynamics for Anthropic as an illustrative microcosm: The Information reported -94% gross margins in 2024 with a target of ~40% in 2025, demonstrating a rapid margin expansion trajectory as serving becomes more efficient. He notes Anthropic’s Sonnet 3.5 was a workhorse for over a year, illustrating longer commercial lifespans for frontier models and the layering effect (new models premium, older models serve cost‑sensitive tiers). Training payback is presented as manageable because model lifespans are longer than bears assume, revenue growth has been outpacing training cost growth, and training efficiency (data curation, synthetic data, architecture) is improving. Catalysts include margin expansion from serving improvements and higher‑margin product offerings; risks include inability to remain frontier or large customers switching to cheaper open‑source deployments for scale.

### GOOG (Google (Gemini)) — Neutral, MENTIONED

Google’s Gemini is named as the third major participant in the labs oligopoly and is treated as part of the competitive set that will shape CAC/training payback dynamics for the market. The author’s framing is that the labs' CAC payback profiles will resemble hyperscalers' payback because the market structure looks like an oligopoly anchored by OpenAI, Anthropic and Gemini: “There’s really 3 that stand out (that are in the business of selling inference tokens themselves). OpenAI, Anthropic and Gemini from Google.” Google’s advantage as a hyperscaler is a structural factor that can compress CAC and create rational pricing dynamics, but the writeup does not opine on Google’s specific unit economics beyond its inclusion in the oligopoly. A principal risk noted is that hyperscaler competition can accelerate feature integration but also raise the bar for frontier performance and margin management.

## Broader Themes

### ai-labs-profitability

The author defends a path to sustained profitability for model labs across three buckets: gross margins, training costs (training payback), and retention/commoditization. Gross margins: labs today mirror early infra software — negative/low initially then expanding toward the 60%–70% range as utilization, region/capacity amortization, serving architecture (prompt caching, tiered storage), and product optimization improve. Training payback: analogized to SaaS CAC payback (rules of thumb: <12 months fantastic; 12–24 months good; 24–36 months OK; >36 months dicey), the author argues training payback is likely short and improving because commercial model lifespans exceed common belief, revenue growth frequently doubles (or more) year‑over‑year while training costs rise ~3–5x per generation, and training efficiency (FLOPs per capability) is improving; “Longer model lifespans than people think, revenue growth outpacing training cost growth, and improving training efficiency.” CAC/retention: the market is an oligopoly — OpenAI, Anthropic, Gemini — which lowers CAC and produces rational pricing and high retention; platform lock‑in (fine‑tunes, eval suites, system prompts, agent ecosystems, committed spend) creates migration costs that are rising. Key risks are open‑source models being used for large deployments (customers prototyping on labs then shifting to cheaper OSS stacks) and the labs failing to remain frontier; catalysts are layerable higher‑margin products and deeper platform features (memory, agents, long‑running workflows).

### saas-valuations

The newsletter’s valuation analysis shows compressed median revenue multiples and substantial dispersion concentrated in a few outliers. Key exacts: median EV / NTM revenue multiple = 3.3x, Top‑5 median = 18.2x, long‑term pre‑COVID average = 7.8x, and the 10‑year Treasury = 4.3%; growth‑bucket medians are High Growth = 10.8x, Mid Growth = 6.4x, Low Growth = 2.6x. Operating/firm metrics across the sample: median NTM growth = 13%, median LTM growth = 15%, median gross margin = 75%, median operating margin = -1%, median FCF margin = 20%, median net retention = 109%, median CAC payback = 34 months, median S&M = 35% of revenue, R&D = 23%, G&A = 15%. The author emphasizes interest‑rate sensitivity: valuation multiples expanded when 10‑year yields fell and compressed as yields rose. Takeaway: a few names command extremely high multiples (right tail), while most firms trade at low single‑digit revenue multiples; FCF multiples also compressed (median EV/NTM FCF ≈ 14.4x vs long‑term average 40.1x).

**Companies referenced:** PLTR, NET, CRWD, CLOUDFLARE, PALANTIR, Figma, CONFLUENT, FASTLY, DATADOG

### quarterly-reports

The author summarizes recent quarterly revenue beats/misses across nine cloud/security/software companies: most names beat reported revenue consensus while guidance was generally in line or slightly above for several. Notable exacts: Rubrik reported $378M vs $342.2M (Δ +10.4%), UiPath $481.1M vs $464.8M (Δ +3.5%), ServiceTitan $254.0M vs $245.2M (Δ +3.6%), Adobe $6,398M vs $6,277M (Δ +1.9%), Sprinklr $220.6M vs $217.0M (Δ +1.7%). Downsides: Yext missed by -1.0% on reported revenue and PagerDuty guided -3.6% below consensus for next quarter. Two companies (Yext, Domo) provided no next‑quarter guidance (NA); the takeaway is revenue execution is broadly healthy but guidance can be uneven.
